The Silo Pattern
Marketing owns Acquire. Product owns Activate. Nobody clearly owns Retain or Referral. Sales & CS span 3 stages but their OKRs are revenue-weighted. The Compass creates shared visibility across stage boundaries — when Marketing generates an MQL, Product sees activation, CS sees health, and Revenue sees conversion — on the same entity.

The Definition Problem

You can’t hit OKRs when teams disagree on what the words mean.

Definitions That Block OKR Progress
“Active customer” — undefined across Product, CS, and Sales. Different meaning per department.
“Inbound” — SDRs say “I don’t consider them inbound” for leads they prospect. Marketing disagrees.
“ARR” — three different figures depending on who you ask (subscription vs total vs bookings).
“MQL” — previously drove volume, not quality. Salespeople booked on irrelevant meetings.
“ICP fit” — 4 departments, 4 ICPs. Sales ignores the official one. SDR targets contradict it.

Hugo: “We usually only have the data itself in mind and not how we use it.”

The Compass’s Library of Definitions forces negotiation. Every metric shows agreed / proposed / disputed status. You can’t argue about numbers when you first have to agree on what the numbers mean.

Summary

What the Compass Makes Possible
For 100M ARR: PCM by market gives leadership a geographic compass. Ghost TAM surfaces untapped demand. Compound health scores protect existing revenue while new markets scale.

For 10K WAU: Shared activation definition means everyone counts the same way. Time-to-first-Find becomes the universal onboarding metric. WAC:PCM ratio shows if engagement converts.

For every OKR: The Library of Definitions eliminates the “whose number is right” arguments. When Marketing’s MQL converts to Sales’s deal converts to CS’s healthy customer — it’s all the same entity, tracked from Acquire to Referral.
