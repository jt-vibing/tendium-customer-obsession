Methodology

Recording Audit

Don’t trust the tracking plan. Watch what users actually do. Use session recordings to build ground-truth definitions of activation, retention, and adoption — then validate any event data against what you see.

The Principle
PostHog event tracking is an abstraction layer. Someone decided “this click = activation” before watching what users actually do. That’s a map drawn before visiting the territory. Session recordings are the territory. Start there.
Why not both?
Recordings = how you discover truth (what does “active” really look like?). Events = how you query truth at scale (is Sigma IT active this week?). You need both — but recordings come first. They define what the events should be.

Step 1: Watch 30 Sessions ~2 hours
Screen-record PostHog session replays and feed them to Claude for analysis. No manual note-taking — Claude watches the recordings and extracts patterns, friction points, and behavioral signals.

Cohort selection (10 sessions each):

Cohort Criteria Why
Healthy High ARR, 12+ months tenure, regular weekly logins What does “good” look like?
At-Risk Low usage last 30 days, approaching renewal, support tickets What does “bad” look like?
New First 14 days after signup (trial or paid) Where do they get stuck or succeed?
How Claude Watches Workflow
PostHog session replays are DOM-based (not video files), so they need to be screen-recorded first. Three options, simplest to most automated:

Option A: Screen record manually
Open PostHog → play a session replay → screen-record with macOS (Cmd+Shift+5) → save as .mov → feed to Claude. Works for 30 sessions.

Option B: Screenshot key moments
Open session replay → pause at key interactions → screenshot each → feed the set to Claude with timestamps. Faster, less storage.

Option C: PostHog event timeline as proxy
Export the session’s event timeline (clicks, pageviews, in order) via PostHog API. Claude reads the structured event sequence instead of watching video. Loses visual context but is fully automatable.

Recommended for V1: Option A for the first 10 sessions (to establish visual patterns), then Option C for the remaining 20 (to scale).

Step 2: What to Look For — By AARRR Stage

For each session recording, Claude maps observed behavior to these 5 lifecycle stages. The goal is to answer: what does each stage actually look like in the product? Not what we think it looks like — what we see.

Acquire Where did they come from?
Look for in recording
  • Entry point: What was the first page they saw? Direct link, Google, referral?
  • First interaction: Did they search, browse, or go straight to signup?
  • Drop-off: Did they leave before signing up? At what point?
  • Procurement signal: Did they arrive because of a tender notification?
Current gap: Visitor → prospect is a black box. Recordings may reveal whether visitors show intent signals (searching for specific tenders, viewing pricing) that aren’t captured in events.
Activate Did they get value?
Look for in recording
  • Time to first value: How many minutes from signup to finding a relevant tender?
  • Setup friction: Did they struggle with filters, onboarding steps, or account config?
  • Aha moment: What was the first thing that made them lean in? A tender result? A digest email?
  • Feature discovery: Did they find BidFlow, Intelligence, or Ask Tendium naturally?
  • Confusion signals: Repeated clicks, back-and-forth navigation, rage clicks
Key question: Is “found a relevant tender” really the activation event, or is it something else? Watch 10 new user recordings and find out.
Retain Did they come back?
Look for in recording
  • Session frequency: Compare healthy vs at-risk — how often do they come back?
  • Session depth: Healthy users — do they go deeper (BidFlow, checklists) or just skim?
  • Usage patterns: Do retained users follow a consistent workflow, or random browsing?
  • Decay signals: At-risk users — did sessions get shorter over time? Fewer pages visited?
  • Habit loop: Is there a trigger (digest email?) → action (search) → reward (tender found) pattern?
Key question: What’s the difference in behavior between a customer at 90% health and one at 40%? The answer defines the health score.
Revenue Did they expand?
Look for in recording
  • Plan ceiling hits: Did they try to create a 2nd filter (Light plan limit)? Hit BidFlow limit?
  • Feature exploration: Did they click into Intelligence features (paywalled for Light)?
  • Multi-user signals: Different users from same company using different features?
  • Pricing page visits: Did they view pricing/upgrade pages? How often?
Key question: What in-product behavior predicts an upsell? This is the signal that connects PostHog to Younium revenue data in the Customer Graph.
Referral Would they recommend?
Look for in recording
  • Power user signals: High session frequency + deep feature usage + long tenure
  • Collaboration features: Did they invite colleagues? Share tenders?
  • Content engagement: Do they use features that generate shareable output (reports, exports)?
Key question: Can we identify “champions” from recordings alone? These are the people to ask for testimonials and referrals.

Step 3: Map Observations to Minimal Events

The Rule
Only track what you’ve seen matter. If you watched 30 recordings and a behavior never appeared, don’t track it. If a behavior clearly separated healthy from at-risk, that’s a must-track event. The tracking plan emerges from observation, not assumption.

Claude Analysis Prompt

Prompt Template for Recording Analysis
When feeding a screen recording or screenshot sequence to Claude, use this prompt:

You are analyzing a PostHog session recording from Tendium’s B2G SaaS product.

User context: [Healthy / At-Risk / New] customer, [plan tier], [tenure]

Watch for and report:
1. Entry & first action — How did they arrive? What did they do first?
2. Value moments — When did they find something useful? What was it?
3. Friction points — Where did they hesitate, go back, or get confused?
4. Feature usage — Which features: Find, BidFlow, Intelligence, Ask Tendium, Digests?
5. Drop-off risk — Any signals they’re disengaging or not finding value?

Output format:
• AARRR stage mapping (which stage is this session primarily in?)
• Key behaviors observed (bullet list)
• Suggested tracking event (if a clear moment should be instrumented)
• Health signal (positive / neutral / negative)

Ongoing: Monthly Audit

After the initial 30 sessions
Watch 5–10 recordings per month to check that event data still matches reality. Tracking plans decay — features change, user behavior evolves, events break silently. The monthly audit catches drift before it corrupts your Customer Graph data.
